Necessary conditions for convergence : Cauchy's sequence

Theorem 8

What is necessary and sufficient for sequence \(\{a_n\}\) to converge is : If for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists a \(n_0\) such that, \begin{equation} p > n_0, q > n_0 \mbox{ then }|a_p - a_q| < \varepsilon . \end{equation}

It is clear that the statement above is necessary : Say \(a_n \to \lambda \), from the definition of convergence; we can choose certain \(n_0\) such that \begin{equation} p > n,q>n, |a_p - \lambda| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2},|a_p - \lambda| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} . \end{equation} Thus \(|a_p -a_q| < \varepsilon\).

What is important is the sufficiency of statement. First, we can confirm that \(\{a_n\}\) is bounded. In fact, if we can choose \(\varepsilon\) according to one \(n_0\); when \(p > n_0\) : \begin{equation} |a_{n_0 + 1} - a_p| < \varepsilon, \\ a_{n_0 + 1} - \varepsilon < a_p < a_{n_0 + 1} + \varepsilon . \end{equation} Thus, \(\{a_n\}\) is bounded for all \(n\) which are larger than \(n_0\). Since we chose appropriate \(n_0\), adding finite numbers \(a_1,a_2,...,a_n\) to \(\{a_n\}\) keeps it bounded.

Therefore, for arbitrary \(n_0\), take \(l_n,m_n\) as supremum and infimum of \(a_n,a_{n+1},...\); and let \(I_n = [l_n,m_n]\) then : \begin{equation} m_1 \le m_2 \le ... \le m_n \le ..... \le l_n \le ... \le l_2 \le l_1 , \tag{1} \\ I_1 \supset I_2 \supset ... \supset I_n \supset ... \mbox{ .} \end{equation}

From the assumption, there exists \(n_0\) for all \(\varepsilon > 0\), when \(p > n_0,q > n_0\) : \begin{equation} a_p - a_q < \varepsilon . \end{equation} Thus, for all \(n > n_0\), from definition of supremum; \(l_n - a_q \le \varepsilon\) for arbitrary \(q \le n\). From definition of infimum, \begin{equation} l_n - m_n \le \varepsilon . \end{equation} From what we discussed on nested intervals, we know that there exists \(\lambda\) such that : \begin{equation} l_n \to \lambda , m_n \to \lambda . \end{equation} Then, it has to be \begin{equation} a_n \to \lambda . \end{equation} In fact, because \(a_n\) belongs to \([m_n,l_n]\); for sufficiently large \(n\) : \begin{equation} |a_n - \lambda | \le l_n - m_n \le \varepsilon \end{equation}

Q.E.D.

Limit superior,Limit inferior

In the proof demonstrated above, (1) was demonstrated only using boundedness of \(\{a_n\}\). Monotonic sequences \(\{l_n\},\{m_n\}\) in (1) converges. Say \(\lambda,\mu\) are limits of these, then \(\lambda\) is said to be limit superior(German: limes superior) of bounded sequence \(\{a_n\}\); that is denoted as \(\limsup_{n\to\infty} a_n\), or \(\varlimsup_{n\to\infty} a_n\). Similarly, we call \(\mu\) limit inferior(German: limes inferior) of \(\{a_n\}\); and denote it \(\liminf_{n\to\infty} a_n\), or \(\varliminf_{n\to\infty} a_n\). As such, bounded sequences \(\{a_n\}\) would always have limit superior \(\lambda\) and limit inferior \(\mu\), and they are \(\lambda \le \mu\). Sequences converge if and only if they are same value : \begin{equation} \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lambda = \mu . \end{equation}

  1. To be limit superior \(\lambda\) requires followings :
    1. With \(\lambda + \varepsilon\) which is greater than \(\lambda\), \(a_n < \lambda + \varepsilon\) for sufficiently large \(n\).
    2. With \(\lambda - \varepsilon\) which is less than \(\lambda\), there exist infinite number of \(n\) such that \(\lambda - \varepsilon < a_n\).

In fact, from the definition of \(\lambda\), \(l_n < \lambda + \varepsilon\) for sufficiently large \(n\), thus \(a_n \le l_n \le \lambda + \varepsilon\). That is what (i) claims. Additionally, as \(l_p\) being superior of \(a_p,a_{p+1},...\); for arbitrary \(p\) there exists \(a_n\) such that \(\lambda -\varepsilon \le l_p - \varepsilon < a_n,n \le p\). Because \(p\) is arbitrary chosen, infinitely many \(a_n\) exists. That is what (ii) claims.

We can describe (a) and (b) differently :

  1. There exist infinite number of \(a_n\) close to \(\lambda\). However, if we take \(\lambda' > \lambda\), there do not.

Or :

  1. There exists a partial sequence of \(\{a_n\}\) that converges to \(\lambda\), however, there is none of which converges to \(\lambda'\) which is larger than \(\lambda\). We call \(\lambda\) limit superior of \(\{a_n\}\), to indicate this characteristics.

For limit inferior, you simply need to invert magnitude relationships in (a),(b),(c).

In practical use, it is convenient to define limit superior and limit inferior for non-bounded sequences. To refer it simpler, we allow use of \(+\infty , -\infty\) in (c). That are to say :

  • \( \{a_n\} \mbox{ is not bounded from above.} \Rightarrow \varlimsup a_n = + \infty \)
  • \( \{a_n\} \mbox{ is not bounded from below.} \Rightarrow \varliminf a_n = - \infty \)
  • \(\{a_n\} \mbox{ is bounded from above, but is not bounded from below.} \) \( \Rightarrow \varliminf a_n = \lambda \mbox{ if } l_n \to \lambda , \varlimsup a_n = - \infty \mbox{ if } l_n \to - \infty . \) \( \mbox{It goes same when } \{a_n\}\) is bounded from below, but is not bounded from above.

Including \(\pm \infty\), arbitrary sequence \(\{a_n\}\) has both \(\varlimsup a_n\) and \(\varliminf a_n \). When they are equal, \(\{a_n\}\) has its limit.

Examples :

  1. \(a_n = \frac{(-1)^n n+1}{n} \Rightarrow \varlimsup a_n = 1,\varliminf a_n = -1\)
  2. \(a_{2n} = 1 + \frac{(-1)^n}{n} , a_{2n+1} = \frac{(-1)^n}{n} \Rightarrow \varlimsup a_n = 1,\varliminf a_n = 0.\) This sequence looks like \(-1,0,\frac{1}{2},\frac{3}{2},-\frac{1}{3},\frac{2}{3},...\). There are several items which are greater than \(1\), and ones which are less than \(0\).
  3. \(a_n = (-1)^n n \Rightarrow \varlimsup a_n = + \infty,\varliminf a_n = - \infty\)
  4. \(a_n = \cos n \alpha \mbox{(choose \(\alpha\) such that \(\pi / \alpha \) is irrational)} \Rightarrow \varlimsup a_n = 1,\varliminf a_n = -1\). It is difficult to prove it. First, take irrational \(\alpha / \pi \). Take arbitrary point \(A\) on circumference of the unit circle. Take point \(P_n\) on circumference of the unit circle, such that length of arc \(A P_n\) is \(n \alpha\).

Sequence in higher dimension

If we take points \(P_n = (x_{1n},x_{2n},...x_{mn}) \) with natural number \(n,m\), sequence of points in m-dimensional space \(\{P_n\}\) can also be defined similarly as 1-dimensional sequence.

Limit of \(\{P_n\}\) is a point in m-dimensional space \((a_1,a_2,...,a_m)\) such that : If for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists sufficiently large \(n_0\) such that, if \(n > n_0\), then \(A P_n < \varepsilon\). If and only if the above statement is satisfied, \(\{P_n\}\) converges to \(A\).

Distance \(A P_m\) is defined as \(\sqrt{(a_1 - x_{1n})^2 + (a_2 - x_{2n})^2 + ... + (a_n - x_{mn})^2 }\). If it is less than \(\varepsilon\), then \(|a - x_n| < \varepsilon , |a - x_{2n}| < \varepsilon , ... ,|a - x_{mn}| < \varepsilon\). Namely, \(AP_n < m\varepsilon\). Hence \(P_n \to A \) is equivalent to \begin{align} \lim_{n \to \infty} x_{1n} = a_1 , \lim_{n \to \infty} x_{2n} = a_2 , ... , \lim_{n \to \infty} x_{mn} = a_m . \end{align}

Finally, we get generalisation of Cauchy's sequence.

Theorem 8

What is necessary and sufficient for sequence of points \(\{P_n\}\) to converge is : If for every \(\varepsilon > 0\) there exists a \(n_0\) such that, \begin{equation} p > n_0, q > n_0 \mbox{ then }P_p P_q < \varepsilon . \end{equation}
